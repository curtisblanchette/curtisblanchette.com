## The engagement

The Atlantic Labs is not a single product. It's an ongoing AI sandbox: an editorial-AI innovation engagement between The Atlantic and Metalab where multiple experiments run in parallel, share infrastructure, and ship to readers only when they earn it.

The brief is bigger than any individual deliverable. **Can a 168-year-old publisher use AI-native surfaces to make its archive more useful — without compromising voice, editorial standards, or reader trust?** That question is the engagement. Specific experiments are the way we answer it in public.

## The pattern

What makes the Labs engagement work — and what I owned as engineering lead — is the operating pattern underneath every experiment:

- **Experiment-as-package.** A Turborepo monorepo, with each experiment scoped as its own app inside `experiments/`. New experiments inherit the toolchain (TypeScript, build, test, deploy) without setup tax. Spinning up the next hypothesis costs hours, not weeks.
- **One CI/CD pipeline, many surfaces.** A single deployment harness handles browser extensions, marketing microsites, internal admin tools, and backend services. The same pipeline that ships a wrapper experiment ships an editorial dashboard.
- **Shared retrieval, swappable ranking.** Every experiment talks to the same recommendation backend. Ranking models are swapped behind a `model` parameter, so editorial can A/B which lens works best for a given experience — *Balanced*, *Timely*, *Long-form*, or anything we add next — without redeploying the experiment.
- **Editorial as a first-class user.** Editorial controls are not a backend tuning knob. They're a product surface, named in plain English, used by The Atlantic's own team to steer what gets surfaced.
- **Privacy by default.** No background page classification. No silent profile-building. The system only acts on context a reader has explicitly given it.

## Experiments shipped under the umbrella

- **The Atlantic Take** — a browser extension that surfaces archival Atlantic stories matched to whatever you're reading on the open web. Three retrieval models, an editorial control surface, a swipeable card UI, and a marketing microsite.
- **Wrapper experiments** — a family of reader-facing surfaces (codenamed *Abby*, *Home*, *Odyssey*) that explored different placements and form factors for AI-driven discovery against the same retrieval backend.

Each experiment was scoped to validate a specific hypothesis, ship in weeks, and either graduate, iterate, or sunset on the evidence.

```
   ┌───────────────────── EDITORIAL ──────────────────────┐

   └────────────────────────┬─────────────────────────────┘
                            │
   ┌────────────────────────▼─────────────────────────────┐
   │  Shared retrieval / ranking service                  │
   │  Atlantic archive index · Balanced / Timely / Long…  │
   └─────┬──────────────┬──────────────┬──────────────────┘
         │              │              │
    ┌────▼───┐     ┌────▼────┐    ┌────▼────────────┐
    │ TAKE   │     │ WRAPPER │    │ WRAPPER         │
    │ (ext.) │     │ (Abby)  │    │ (Home/Odyssey)  │
    └────────┘     └─────────┘    └─────────────────┘
```

A Terraform-managed AWS footprint underneath. A Turborepo monorepo on top. Everything in between is the experiment harness.

## What I owned

- Technical lead across the engagement — not any single experiment.
- The experiment harness: monorepo layout, shared infrastructure, the CI/CD pipeline that lets a new experiment go from `git init` to deployed in days.
- The contract between editorial, the retrieval/ranking service, and every reader-facing surface.
- AWS infrastructure via Terraform; the deployment story The Atlantic could operate without us.
- The editorial-surface design — translating "what makes a good match for *this* audience" between domain experts and the ranking team into a named, controllable product surface.

## What carried forward

The most useful pattern from Labs — the one that will outlive any specific experiment — is **treating the editorial control surface as a first-class product**, not a tuning knob. The moment the model choice (Balanced / Timely / Long-form) was named, surfaced, and controllable, the conversation about quality moved from *"is the AI right?"* to *"which lens do we want for this reader?"*. A much better conversation to have. A version of this thinking shows up in [The Quiet Trade](/writing/the-quiet-trade): judgment is the priced layer; the deliverable should make that judgment visible, not hide it.
